## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:

1. Derive the Euler equation for consumption growth in the RCK model using the Hamiltonian and interpret it as the modified golden rule
2. Construct the phase diagram with the $\dot{k}=0$ and $\dot{c}/c=0$ loci and explain the saddle-path dynamics
3. Show that the decentralized competitive equilibrium replicates the social planner's solution and state the conditions under which this result breaks down
4. Explain why Ricardian equivalence holds with lump-sum taxes and infinite horizons
5. Derive the effect of a capital income tax on the steady-state capital stock and explain why a lump-sum rebate does not restore efficiency
6. Determine the sign of the saving-growth relationship in the RCK model as a function of risk aversion

## Key Concepts

- **Modified golden rule (Keynes-Ramsey rule)**: the Euler equation $\dot{c}/c = \rho^{-1}[f'(k) - \delta - \delta_t - n - \rho g]$ that governs consumption growth along the transition path
- **Golden rule capital stock**: the level of $k$ that maximizes steady-state consumption, where $f'(k^{GR}) = n + g + \delta$; the RCK steady state falls short of the golden rule when consumers are impatient
- **Saddle path**: the unique trajectory in the $(k, c)$ plane that satisfies both the Euler equation and the transversality condition, converging to the steady state
- **First Welfare Theorem**: in the RCK model without externalities or distortionary taxes, the decentralized equilibrium is Pareto optimal
- **Ricardian equivalence**: with infinite horizons and lump-sum taxes, the timing of government taxation has no effect on consumption or capital accumulation
- **Capital income tax distortion**: a tax on capital income reduces the after-tax return to saving and lowers the steady-state capital stock, even when revenue is rebated lump-sum
